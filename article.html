Southern Africa Faces New Economic Challenges as Global Markets Shift

By Staff Reporter 📅 March 22, 2026 ⏱ 5 min read 📍 Maseru, Lesotho

Regional economists gathered in Maseru to address the impact of global economic shifts on Southern African nations. Photo: Mpuse's News Portal

Regional economists and policy makers gathered in Maseru this week for a two-day summit focused on the growing impact of global market changes on the economies of Southern Africa. The conference, organized jointly by the governments of Lesotho, South Africa, and Zimbabwe, brought together over 120 delegates from across the continent.

The summit comes at a critical time. Rising inflation, shifting trade routes, and the increasing cost of imported goods have placed significant pressure on smaller economies in the region. Lesotho, like many of its neighbors, remains heavily dependent on imports and remittances from citizens working abroad.

"We cannot afford to wait for global markets to stabilize before acting. The time for unified regional policy is now."

— Dr. Lineo Mofokeng, Economist, National University of Lesotho

Key Issues Discussed

Delegates identified three main areas of concern. First, the cost of food imports has increased by an estimated 18% over the past year, placing strain on low-income households. Second, the textile and manufacturing sectors — which employ thousands of workers in Lesotho — face competition from cheaper alternatives emerging in Asian markets. Third, access to affordable financing for small and medium-sized enterprises remains a persistent barrier to economic growth.

South Africa's Deputy Minister of Trade and Industry called on member states of the Southern African Development Community (SADC) to fast-track a new regional investment framework that would allow businesses to operate across borders with reduced bureaucracy and tariffs.

Gallery: Summit in Pictures

What Happens Next

Following the summit, participating governments have committed to producing a joint policy brief within 60 days. The document will include recommendations on trade facilitation, agricultural self-sufficiency, and digital economic development.

Civil society organizations present at the summit welcomed the commitments but warned that implementation must be closely monitored. "We have seen many good plans on paper before. What matters is follow-through," said one representative from a regional business association.

The next summit is scheduled for June 2026 in Harare, Zimbabwe, where the policy brief will be formally presented to heads of state.
